1.1 Physical Quantities

  1. Physical quantities are quantities that can be measured.
    Examples of physical quantities: Length, mass, time, temperature and electric current.

  2. A physical quantity consists of a numerical magnitude with a unit.
    Example: Mass of the Sun = 1 989 000 000 000 000 000 000 000 000 000 kg
    = 1.989 x 10$^{30}$ kg

​ ↓ ↓ ↓

  1. A standard form is a way of writing numbers, in which A is multiplied by an appropriate power, $n$, of 10 as follows:
    A × 10$^n$ where 1 ≤ A < 10 and $n$ is an integer

  2. Very small or very large numerical magnitudes or values should be expressed in the standard form to the powers of 10 as follows:

    Example

  4. Smaller or larger units are formed by adding one of the prefixes to the units.

    Example:

    1000 m = 10$^3$ m = 1 km (kilometre - prefix kilo, k added to metre)

    0.000003 s = 3 × 10$^{-6}$ s = 3 μs (microsecond - prefix micro, μ added to second) . Prefix, μ

  5. Since 1968, scientists agreed to adopt the International System of Units (S.I. units) internationally for all scientific measurements. S.l. units are based on metric units. However, another set of units that is commonly used in science and daily life is the system of imperial units.

    Example:
